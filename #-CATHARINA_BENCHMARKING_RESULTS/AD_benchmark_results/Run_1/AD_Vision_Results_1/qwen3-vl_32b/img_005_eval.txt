LLM RESPONSE:
### 1. Observation  
- **Account lockout duration**: 5 minutes  
- **Account lockout threshold**: 5 invalid logon attempts  
- **Reset account lockout counter after**: 5 minutes  

### 2. Assessment  
**Misconfigured**.  
While the lockout threshold (5 failed attempts) is acceptable, the **5-minute lockout duration** and **5-minute reset counter** create a critical weakness. The short lockout duration allows attackers to repeatedly brute-force accounts (5 attempts every 5 minutes), and the reset counter value matching the lockout duration enables immediate re-attack after unlock. This violates Microsoft's best practices for preventing brute-force attacks.  

### 3. Attack Path  
- **Attack Vector**: **Brute Force Attack**.  
- **Technical Impact**:  
  - An attacker can make 5 invalid logon attempts → account locks for 5 minutes.  
  - After 5 minutes, the lockout ends *and* the failed attempt counter resets (due to identical 5-minute settings).  
  - The attacker can then immediately launch another 5 attempts (repeating the cycle).  
  - This allows **100+ failed attempts per hour** (e.g., 12 sets of 5 attempts in 60 minutes), enabling password cracking against weak credentials. The short duration also bypasses "lockout-based" detection mechanisms.  

### 4. Remediation  
**Step-by-step fix for the Group Policy Object (GPO)**:  
1. Open **Group Policy Management Console (GPMC)**.  
2. Locate the GPO (e.g., "Default Domain Policy") containing the Account Lockout Policy settings.  
3. Edit the GPO:  
   - **Computer Configuration** > **Policies** > **Windows Settings** > **Security Settings** > **Account Policies** > **Account Lockout Policy**.  
4. Adjust the settings:  
   - **Account lockout duration**: Set to **30 minutes** (minimum; 1 hour is preferred for critical environments).  
   - **Reset account lockout counter after**: Set to **30 minutes** (must be ≥ lockout duration; e.g., 30 minutes).  
   - *Optional*: Increase **Account lockout threshold** to **10 invalid logon attempts** (if business continuity allows).  
5. **Apply changes**:  
   - Click **OK** to save.  
   - Force GPO update: Run `gpupdate /force` on domain controllers.  
6. **Verify**:  
   - Test with a test user: 5 failed logons should lock the account for 30 minutes.  
   - Ensure no new failed attempts reset the counter before the lockout duration expires.  

> **Why this fix?** A 30-minute lockout duration (and matching reset counter) significantly increases the time between attack cycles, deterring automated brute-force tools. Microsoft recommends ≥30 minutes for lockout duration (see [MS 2020 Security Baseline](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/security/threat-protection/security-policy-recommendations)). The 5-minute settings are *not* secure per industry standards.
