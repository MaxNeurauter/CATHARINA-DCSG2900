- **"Everyone"** and **"Authenticated Users"** having **"Special"** permissions on the **Domain Admins** group is **extremely dangerous**.
- This allows **any authenticated user** (including low-privileged accounts) to **enumerate and potentially manipulate** the group.

#### **B. A non-admin user ("lord.varys") having Full Control**
- **"lord.varys"** (likely a standard user) has **Full Control** over the **Domain Admins** group.
- This means an attacker who compromises this account can **add themselves to Domain Admins**, gaining **full domain dominance**.

#### **C. Pre-Windows 2000 Compatible Access**
- This legacy permission can allow **unnecessary access** and should be removed unless explicitly required.

### **3. Attack Path**
#### **A. Privilege Escalation (Domain Takeover)**
1. An attacker compromises **"lord.varys"** (via phishing, credential dumping, or another exploit).
2. Since **"lord.varys"** has **Full Control** over **Domain Admins**, the attacker **adds themselves** to the group.
3. The attacker now has **full administrative control** over the domain.

#### **B. Lateral Movement & Enumeration**
1. An attacker with a low-privileged account (e.g., via **Pass-the-Hash** or **Kerberoasting**) can **enumerate Domain Admins** due to **"Authenticated Users"** having **"Special"** permissions.
2. The attacker can then **target high-value accounts** (e.g., **Enterprise Admins, Domain Admins**) for further compromise.

#### **C. Silver Ticket & Golden Ticket Attacks**
- If an attacker gains access to **NTLM hashes** (via **Mimikatz** or **DCSync**), they can **forge Kerberos tickets** (Silver/Golden Tickets) to impersonate **Domain Admins** and **Enterprise Admins**.
