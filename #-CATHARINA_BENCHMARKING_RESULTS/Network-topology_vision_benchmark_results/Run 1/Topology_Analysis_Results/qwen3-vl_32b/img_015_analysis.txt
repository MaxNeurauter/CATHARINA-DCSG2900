### 4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)  
- **Critical SPOF (Single Point of Failure):**  
  - Only **one router** connects the Campus Backbone to the WAN (left side of the diagram). A failure here would isolate the entire Enterprise Campus.  
  - The "Campus Backbone" (left) shows only a *single router* linking to the WAN, with no HA (High Availability) pairing.  
- **Lack of Security Boundaries:**  
  - **No firewalls/ASAs** are visible between the Campus Backbone and WAN. This exposes the internal network to WAN threats.  
  - Remote Sites (e.g., Branch Office, Telecommuter) connect directly to the WAN without security appliances (e.g., ASA or NGFW).  
- **Inefficient Redundancy:**  
  - The Campus Backbone’s "X" router (left) has **only one WAN link** (red arrow), but the WAN cloud itself has redundant routers. This misalignment creates a SPOF.  
- **No DMZ for Remote Access:** Telecommuters/remote users connect directly to the WAN without a secure DMZ or VPN gateway, increasing exposure.  

---

### 5. Recommendations  
1. **Implement HA Pairs at Campus-WAN Edge:**  
   - Deploy **two routers** (e.g., Cisco 4500 series) in an **HSRP/VRRP pair** at the Campus Backbone-WAN boundary to eliminate SPOF. Use 2x parallel WAN links for redundancy.  
   - *Why?* Aligns with Cisco best practices and ensures no single failure disrupts campus-WAN connectivity.  

2. **Add Security Appliance at WAN Edge:**  
   - Place a **firewall (e.g., Cisco ASA/Firepower)** between the Campus Backbone and WAN cloud to enforce security policies, inspect traffic, and segment internal vs. external networks.  
   - *Why?* Critical for preventing unauthorized access and compliance (e.g., PCI, GDPR).  

3. **Enhance VLAN Segmentation & DMZ:**  
   - Use **VLANs** to segment workgroups, servers, and management traffic within the Campus Backbone. Create a **DMZ** for remote access (e.g., telecommuter/VPN) with a separate security zone.  
   - *Why?* Reduces broadcast domains, isolates threats, and meets NIST 800-53 security requirements.
