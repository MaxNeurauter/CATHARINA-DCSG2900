### 2. Purpose & Architecture  
The design is a **basic segmented network with a DMZ** for hosting public services (e.g., web servers). The LAN isolates internal users from the Internet, while the DMZ acts as a buffer zone for servers requiring external access (e.g., HTTP/HTTPS). The router serves as the **bottleneck for all traffic** (Internet ↔ LAN ↔ DMZ).  

---

### 3. Strengths (Pros)  
- **Logical Segmentation:** The DMZ isolates public-facing servers from the internal LAN, reducing the attack surface.  
- **Layer 2 Efficiency:** Switches (2960-24TT) provide local broadcast domain segmentation for LAN/DMZ traffic.  
- **Simplicity:** The design is easy to manage for small-scale deployments (e.g., SOHO or small branch offices).  

---

### 4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)  
- **Single Point of Failure (SPOF):**  
  - **Router1 is the critical SPOF.** If it fails, *all* Internet access, LAN-DMZ communication, and DMZ-to-Internet traffic ceases.  
  - *No redundant routers or failover mechanisms (e.g., HSRP/VRRP).*  
- **Inadequate Security Boundaries:**  
  - **No firewall between router and LAN/DMZ.** The router (2901) is *not* a dedicated security appliance (e.g., Cisco ASA). It lacks advanced features like stateful inspection, IDS/IPS, or granular ACLs for DMZ isolation.  
  - *DMZ traffic is routed directly to the router; if compromised, the LAN could be exposed.*  
- **Limited Scalability & Redundancy:**  
  - The single 2960 switch per segment creates a SPOF for LAN/DMZ.  
  - No VLAN segmentation (e.g., separate VLANs for PCs, servers, or management) within LAN/DMZ.  
  - The router’s limited 2901 platform may not handle high throughput for enterprise-scale traffic.  

---

### 5. Recommendations  
1. **Add a Dedicated Firewall for Security Boundaries:**  
   - Deploy a **firewall (e.g., Cisco ASA)** between the Internet and Router1. Place a *second firewall* between Router1 and the LAN/DMZ switches. This enforces stateful inspection, granular ACLs, and isolates the DMZ from the LAN (e.g., no direct LAN-DMZ traffic without firewall rules).  
   - *Rationale:* Eliminates reliance on router-based ACLs for security and aligns with the **"Security in Depth"** principle.  

2. **Implement High Availability (HA) for Critical Components:**  
   - **Replace the single 2901 router with an HA pair** (e.g., two 2901 routers with HSRP). Deploy redundant 2960 switches with STP (or stack them) to eliminate switch SPOFs.  
   - *Rationale:* Prevents SPOFs at the core, ensuring uninterrupted Internet and inter-segment connectivity.  

3. **Introduce VLAN Segmentation (per Cisco 3-Tier Model):**  
   - On Switch1 (LAN), create **separate VLANs** for users (e.g., VLAN 10), management (VLAN 100), and future services. On Switch2 (DMZ), isolate server workloads into distinct VLANs (e.g., web servers in VLAN 20, database servers in VLAN 30).  
   - *Rationale:* Enhances security (e.g., prevents lateral movement) and simplifies traffic management without requiring new hardware.
