---

### 4. **Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)**  
- **Critical SPOF (Single Point of Failure):**  
  - **SW0 (2960-24TTT)  
- **Lack of Firewall for Internal Network:**  
  - The internal network (PCs, wireless) connects **directly to SW0** without a firewall. This exposes internal traffic to the DMZ and Internet via the unsecured path (SW0 → ASA2 → ASA1 → DMZ).  
  - *Risk:* DMZ breaches could directly impact internal hosts (e.g., malware spreading from a compromised server to internal PCs).  
- **Inefficient Routing:**  
  - The two routers (Router0/1) are not paired for high availability (e.g., HSRP). While two uplinks to the Internet exist, there’s no active/active routing or failover mechanism. If one router fails, traffic must re-route, causing downtime.  
- **No VLAN Segmentation:**  
  - All endpoints (PCs, wireless, DMZ) share the same broadcast domain on SW0. This increases broadcast traffic, security risks, and makes traffic management difficult.  

---

### 5. **Recommendations**  
1. **Implement VLANs and Firewall Policies:**  
   - Create dedicated VLANs (e.g., VLAN 10 for PCs, VLAN 20 for wireless, VLAN 30 for DMZ).

2. **Introduce High Availability (HA) for Core Components:**  
   - **Deploy a second core switch** (e.g., pair SW0 with a new switch) for redundancy. Use LAG (Link Aggregation) to connect both core switches to the routers.  
   - **Configure HSRP on Router0/1** to provide seamless failover for Internet connectivity.  

3. **Optimize the DMZ and Security Boundaries:**  
   - Use **two ASAs in a failover pair** (e.g., ASA1 for DMZ, ASA2 for internal network) to eliminate SPOFs.  
   - Place the DMZ switch (Switch3) behind **a dedicated firewall segment** to enforce strict access control (e.g., "DMZ only" traffic allowed from ASA1, no internal traffic).  

---
