### 4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)  
- **Single Points of Failure (SPOF)**:  
  - **Router1**: Failure of Router1 would isolate both the LAN and DMZ from the Internet, crippling the entire network.  
  - **Switches**: Each switch (Switch0 for DMZ, Switch1 for LAN) is an SPOF; failure of either would disconnect all devices on that segment.  
- **Inadequate Security Boundaries**:  
  - The router is used as the sole boundary device but lacks explicit firewalling (no dedicated firewall like Cisco ASA). Reliance on the router’s ACLs for security is risky—misconfigured ACLs could leave the LAN exposed to DMZ servers or vice versa.  
- **No Redundancy**: Zero redundancy for core devices (router) or segment access (switches), increasing outage risk and recovery time.  
- **Lack of Logical Segmentation**: No VLANs are depicted, so broadcast domains are physically segmented but not logically isolated. This risks broadcast storms and limits fine-grained security policies (e.g., per-traffic filtering).
