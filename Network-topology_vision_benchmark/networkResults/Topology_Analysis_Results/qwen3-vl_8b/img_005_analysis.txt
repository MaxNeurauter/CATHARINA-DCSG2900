### 4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)  
- **Single Points of Failure (SPOF)**:  
  - Router0(4) is the **only Internet-facing device**; failure halts all external connectivity.  
  - ASA0(3) is the **only firewall**; failure isolates internal networks from the Internet *and* renders DMZ/LAN communication impossible (if ASA0 handles routing).  
  - Switch0(1) and Switch1(1) are **unredundant switches**; failure of either segment (DMZ or LAN) cuts off all services for that zone.  
- **Inefficient Topology & Scalability**:  
  - The design lacks **VLAN segmentation** or Layer 3 routing (both Switch0 and Switch1 are Layer 2 switches). This results in a flat network where broadcast domains span the entire LAN/DMZ, increasing collision risks and reducing performance in larger deployments.  
  - No **redundant path** between segments (e.g., no failover for Internet, firewall, or switch connectivity), violating high-availability principles for enterprise networks.  
- **Security Gaps**:  
  - While the DMZ is segmented, **no explicit ACLs** are shown to restrict traffic between DMZ and LAN (e.g., only HTTP/HTTPS from DMZ to LAN). This risks exposing internal LAN resources to untrusted DMZ servers.  
  - ASA0 lacks **failover redundancy** or advanced security features (e.g., IPS/WAF), limiting defense against complex threats.  
- **Resource Overload**:  
  - ASA0 handles all traffic between Internet, DMZ, and LAN, creating a bottleneck and increasing risk of performance degradation. Without Layer 3 aggregation, it also lacks scalability for future growth.
