### **4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)**
❌ **Single Point of Failure (SPOF) – Router1**
   - If **Router1 fails**, both LAN and DMZ lose connectivity to the Internet and each other.
   - No redundancy (e.g., no backup router or failover mechanism).

❌ **Lack of VLAN Segmentation**
   - The LAN and DMZ are likely on the same broadcast domain (unless VLANs are configured, which is not shown).
   - No separation between different departments or security zones within the LAN.

❌ **No Firewall or Advanced Security Controls**
   - The router may have basic firewall rules, but there is **no explicit firewall/ASA** for granular traffic filtering between LAN, DMZ, and Internet.
   - Servers in the DMZ are directly exposed to the router without additional security layers.

❌ **No Redundant Links**
   - If the link between **Switch1 (LAN) and Router1** fails, the entire LAN is isolated.
   - Similarly, if the link between **Switch0 (DMZ) and Router1** fails, DMZ servers lose connectivity.

❌ **Potential Broadcast Storms (Layer 2 Loops)**
   - If misconfigured, **Switch1 and Switch0** could introduce loops if connected improperly (though not shown in this diagram).

### **5. Recommendations (Industry Best Practices)**
#### **1. Introduce Redundancy & High Availability (HA)**
   - **Deploy a redundant router** (e.g., HSRP/VRRP failover) to eliminate the SPOF.
   - **Add redundant links** between switches and routers (e.g., EtherChannel for Switch1-Router1 and Switch0-Router1).
   - **Implement Spanning Tree Protocol (STP)** to prevent Layer 2 loops.

#### **2. Implement VLAN Segmentation & Firewall Rules**
   - **Create VLANs** for different departments (e.g., VLAN 10 for HR, VLAN 20 for Finance) within the LAN.
   - **Use a dedicated firewall/ASA** between LAN and DMZ (instead of relying solely on the router).
