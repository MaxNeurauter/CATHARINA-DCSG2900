#### **3. Strengths (Pros)**  
- **Partial Firewall Protection**: ASA2 correctly secures Segment A (PC0/PC1) from the Internet.  
- **Segmentation Attempt**: Two logical segments (A and B) reduce broadcast domain size compared to a single flat network.  

---

#### **4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)**  
- **Critical Security Gap**:  
  - Segment B (Switch2) is **directly connected to Router0**, meaning all traffic between Switch2 and the Internet bypasses ASA2.  
  - *Impact*: PC2/PC3/Server0 are exposed to unsanctioned traffic (e.g., malware, DDoS).  
- **Single Points of Failure (SPOF)**:  
  - **Router0** fails → Both segments lose Internet connectivity.  
  - **Switch0** fails → Segment A (PC0/PC1) loses connectivity.  
  - **Switch2** fails → Segment B (PC2/PC3/Server0) loses connectivity.  
- **Lack of VLAN Segmentation**: Broadcast domains are uncontrolled (no VLANs), leading to excessive broadcast traffic and poor traffic management.  
- **No Redundancy**: No backup paths or failover mechanisms for routers/firewalls.  

---

#### **5. Recommendations**  

##### **A. Secure All Internal Segments with Firewall Protection**  
- **Reconfigure the Network**:  
  - **Router0 → ASA2** (Internet-facing interface).  
  - **ASA2 → Switch0, Switch2** (internal interfaces).  
  - **Remove direct Router0-Switch2 connection**.  
- **Why?**  
  - Ensures **all internal segments** (A and B) are protected by ASA2.  
  - Prevents Segment B from being exposed to the Internet.  
  - Aligns with **best practice**: Firewalls should be the *only* gateway between the Internet and internal networks.  

##### **B. Implement High Availability (HA) for Critical Components**  
- **Router0**: Deploy an HA pair (e.g., HSRP/VRRP) to eliminate SPOF.  
- **ASA2**: Configure stateful failover to ensure firewall redundancy.  
- **Why?**
