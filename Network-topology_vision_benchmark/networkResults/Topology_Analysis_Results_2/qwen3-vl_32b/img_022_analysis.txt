### 2. Purpose & Architecture  
The intended design with a central switch (Switch3) as a SPOF for all connected segments.  

### 3. Strengths (Pros)  
- **DMZ Isolation**: Clear separation of external-facing servers (*Server0*, *Server1*) via firewalls (*ASA0*, *ASA1*), reducing risk to internal systems.  
- **Internet Redundancy**: Two routers (*Router0*, *Router1*) provide failover capability for internet connectivity.  
- **Wireless Support**: Two access points enable wireless access for laptops, aligning with modern network needs.  
- **Logical Grouping**: Endpoints (PCs, printers), wireless, and DMZ are visually segmented, aiding management.  

### 4. Weaknesses & Risks (Cons)  
- **Critical SPOFs**:  
  - **Switch3** is a single point of failure (SPOF) for *all* segments (internal, wireless, DMZ). A failure here disrupts the entire network.  
  - **ASA Chain**: *ASA0* and *ASA1* are in series. A failure at either ASA or the inter-ASA link **fully isolates the DMZ**.  
  - **Router Links**: Both *Router0* and *Router1* depend on the same two switches (*Switch4*, *Switch5*), creating a shared SPOF for internet access.  
- **Security Gaps**:  
  - No VLANs (as per diagram), leaving all endpoints in a flat broadcast domain (e.g., PCs and printers share the same network segment), increasing lateral attack risk.  
- **Operational Risks**:  
  - Excessive inter-switch links (e.g., *Switch4*–*Switch5* cross-connections) create potential switching loops without proper Spanning Tree Protocol (STP) configuration.  

### 5. Recommendations  
1. **Implement VLAN Segmentation**:  
   - Create VLANs for: *PCs* (VLAN 10), *Printers* (VLAN 20), *Wireless* (VLAN 30), and *DMZ* (VLAN 40). This isolates traffic, reduces broadcast domains, and enhances security.  
2. **Eliminate SPOF at Core**:  
   - Replace *Switch3* with a redundant core (e.g., two *2960-24TT* switches in an active-active configuration). This distributes load and removes the single-point risk.  
3. **Redesign DMZ Firewall Architecture**:  
   - Deploy *ASA0* and *ASA1* as a **High Availability (HA) pair** (e.g., active/standby) with a single interface to the internal network and DMZ. This eliminates the SPOF of the ASA chain.  

By addressing these gaps, the network will achieve better security, resilience, and operational efficiency while aligning with industry best practices.
